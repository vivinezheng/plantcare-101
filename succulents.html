Succulents

The term succulent refers not to a genetic classification of plants, like cactus, but instead refers to plants that have evolved thick and fleshy leaves that are built for water storage. In fact, many members of Cactaceae (cacti) are classified as succulents. Unlike most plants, succulents are known for undergoing a modified kind of photosynthesis called CAM, in which the stomata, tiny holes in the plant leaves, open at night instead of during the day, to prevent water from escaping in the hot sun of their native regions. These plants require very little water, and many will be fine for weeks at a time without any moisture. You should let your soil dry completely in between watering your succulent. If you're unsure if the soil is dry all the way through, you can poke a stick down to the bottom of the pot, and if it has any dirt stuck to it, it still needs to dry. Some sources even suggest to wait until early signs of water deprivation, like wilting leaves, before watering your succulent.

Crassulaceae

The family Crassulaceae, also known as stonecrops, are characterized by their thick fleshy leaves, and many are grouped as succulents. In fact, CAM, or Crassulacean acid metabolism, the special kind of photosynthesis that is employed by succulents, is named after this family. To further aid water collection, these plants have developed aerial roots, which grow above the ground on the plant stem where they can absorb water from the air. Popular members of this family include Crassula ovata (jade plant), Kalanchoe, Echeveria, and Sempervivum. Learn how to care for your Crassulaceae at the link above.
